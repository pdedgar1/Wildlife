Interdisciplinary Workshop — Tropical Field Photography

Thirty images, three arguments

A single field trip through tropical Central America. The photographs surface three unresolved tensions — in evolution, ecology, and in the idea of "nature" itself. Each pattern cuts across taxa, habitats, and disciplines.

Pattern I

Hide or advertise

The glasswing butterfly is near-invisible — its wings are transparent down to the physics of light. The red-eyed tree frog is a neon sign. Every organism here has made the same evolutionary bet, just differently: blend in, or stand out. Two opposite survival strategies shaped by the same predation pressure.

Pattern II

The night shift

More than half these photographs were taken after dark. The daytime community of birds and iguanas steps aside for moths, frogs, spiders, and caterpillars. A different set of actors, on the same stage, using the same resources. The forest doesn't sleep — it changes shift.

16 images · nocturnal community
Pattern III

Where does nature end?

A sloth in an undisturbed canopy. An iguana by a parked motorcycle. A howler monkey on a utility wire. A pig on a tourist beach. These images trace a gradient. The boundary between wild and domestic, between nature and culture, turns out not to be a wall.
